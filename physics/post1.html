Core Principles

Special relativity is based on two postulates proposed by Albert Einstein in 1905:

  • The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames of reference.
  • The speed of light in a vacuum is the same for all observers, regardless of the motion of the light source or observer.

Time Dilation

One of the most famous consequences of special relativity is time dilation. If an object moves at a velocity \(v\) relative to an observer, the time \(t'\) measured in the moving frame is related to the time \(t\) in the observer's frame by:

\[ t' = \frac{t}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}} \]

This means that a moving clock ticks more slowly compared to a stationary one.

Length Contraction

Likewise, the length of an object in motion appears contracted to a stationary observer. The contracted length \(L\) is given by:

\[ L = L_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}} \]

where \(L_0\) is the proper length (length in the object's rest frame).
